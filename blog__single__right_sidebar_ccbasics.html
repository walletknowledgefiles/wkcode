Credit Card Basics

If used responsibly, credit cards can be a great help in running your personal finances. They can help you budget, tide you over tough times, and even enable you to gather a significant amount of reward benefits. But if used irresponsibly, they can be a burden that causes you to pile up unmanageable debt that can result in you running into major financial problems. The key is to use them wisely and let them become your friend rather than your foe. In this article we will guide you through credit-card basics 101 and help you to understand the cards, avoid the pitfalls, and use them to your benefit. If you do, the result will be improved budgeting, less financial stress and even added cash or other benefits.

How credit cards work

Once you obtain a card, you can buy almost anything you like at outlets that accept it. The amounts you spend are paid by the bank and recorded on your account. At the end of a set period, usually close to a month, everything you have spent during that time is totaled. The total amount becomes due by you for payment on a certain day, called the “due date” — usually about another month later. On the due date you need to pay at least the minimum amount set by the bank. For any amount that is unpaid over the minimum, the bank will charge you interest, which, depending on your financial standing, can be high. After the month-long period ends, a new period starts over which your spending is recorded, which follows the same cycle. Most cards have a total spending limit; it might be as low as $500 or as high as $15,000. Some cards have no upper limit. Debit cards are different. In that case, the amount of the sale is deducted immediately from your bank account. You cannot pay it later. You do not incur interest charges or fees at any time.

Credit-card benefits

The next step in understanding credit-card basics 101 is to discover how these cards can help you. • Using cards responsibly enables you to build up a good record with the credit-rating bureaus. Paying on time every month — it does not necessarily have to be the total due — counts in your favor as it indicates that you are using your money responsibly. • Among the other credit-card benefits is that you have time to dispute charges. Because you are paying later, no money is drawn from your account until the dispute is settled — if you are still in the grace period before payment is due. • If you pay your monthly balance in full, you will, in effect, be receiving an interest-free loan on the money during the time before the payment is due. • Should fraudulent charges be made on your cards, you will not be charged the amount as long as the issuer becomes aware that they are false. • In addition, many cards provide extended warranties, cover the insurance on rental cars, and provide insurance on flights.

Credit-card drawbacks

Inevitably, perhaps, the benefits are accompanied by drawbacks, most of which result from irresponsible use of the cards. • Failure to pay at least the minimum due on time will harm your credit-bureau rating, making it really difficult for you to borrow money in the future. • It is easy to run up a large amount of credit-card debt, which continues to grow at a high interest rate month after month. Payment becomes increasingly difficult until it becomes unbearable and can result in major financial problems. The best way to avoid such problems is to pay off your credit-card bill each month. Many card issuers offer the ability for you to have the balance owing each month automatically deducted from your checking or savings account. All you have to do is to make sure the amount is in your account at the time it is deducted.

Credit-card rewards

Most cards offer perks or bonuses for using them. Here’s a look at some of those that are available, how they work and how you can take advantage of them. Remember that most awards are subject to a maximum spending limit over a quarter or a year.

Points

When you spend money, many cards offer you points that accumulate and can result in some sort of reward when they reach a certain amount. The point system might be based on one point for each dollar spent, for example. Accumulation of a certain number of points means the potential to earn some sort of reward. Points can earn a wide variety of free services or products, depending on the nature of the cards. A hotel chain, for example, might award free hotel nights upon accumulation of a specific number of points. Others offer gift cards, merchandise or travel discounts.

Cash back

Among the popular credit-card rewards is the ability to accumulate sufficient points to receive cash back, which enables the users who earn the points to spend the money that they earn when and wherever they like. The cash is usually a percentage of the amount spent — therefore every $100 spent would earn $1 if the card offers 1 percent cash back. In most cases, the user can request that the cash be paid into the user’s checking or savings account. Some banks offer a bonus if the cash is paid back into an account that the user might have at the same bank as the one that issues the cards. In other cases, the cash is paid back in the form of statement credits that are used toward future credit-card purchases with that issuer. Be aware that some cards that offer cash back carry an annual fee. In order to make them attractive, you will need to earn enough points to pay the fee before accumulating more points. Whether such cards are valuable to you depends partly on your buying pattern and how much you spend on the categories for which cash back is offered.

Variable points and Rotating Categories

Some cards offer as much as 5 percent cash back on a quarterly basis. In one quarter, the issuer will offer 5 percent on gas and restaurant spending, for example. A good idea is to have several cards with different variables available each quarter. Under this scenario one of the cards will offer 5 percent on groceries this quarter, while another credit-card issuer will provide a similar offer the following quarter, enabling the user to obtain 5 percent on groceries for a full six months. Other cards offer higher percentages on certain categories at all times, such as 3 percent on restaurants and gas, for example.

Balance transfers

For those who are suffering under high interest rates on their cards, some issuers will offer zero or low percent interest rates on balances that are transferred to their cards. This low rate can be of great help to people who can pay back the balance within the time frame set by the issuer, but if the balance remains high at the end of the zero-rate period, the balance will attract regular interest rates set by the issuer. It is good to be aware, too, that a percentage, such as 3 percent or 4 percent, is often charged on the balance transfer, thereby reducing the benefit somewhat. Be sure and read the fine print to determine whether this is the case in any transfer offer you consider taking.

Travel miles

These cards usually are associated with the name of an airline. Every time you spend money using these cards, you will earn a certain number of “miles,” or points. Those miles can be used toward air fares. Most travel cards also can be used for hotel stays or even rental cars.

Cards with a zero percent introductory loan rate

These cards are similar to those that apply to cards with a zero or low balance transfer rate; the difference, however, is that you start with no money and build up a balance over the period of the introductory rate.

Cards for people with bad credit

Those who are refused cards and cannot find a way to achieve good credit-bureau ratings might want to consider cards that enable a user to pay in a certain amount in advance toward a credit-card balance. Use of the cards responsibly earns the user an improved rating. Jump to top

Gift cards

Another among the most popular forms of rewards are gift cards. The points you accumulate can be used to obtain gift cards that can be used to buy merchandise, meals at restaurants, and so on. Many people give family or friends these gift cards, particularly during the festive season.

Small business cards

These cards enable you to separate your business expenses from your personal expenses. They also usually award points for purchases that are often made by small businesses, such as office supplies or the Internet.
